Land Cover Change from 1984 to 2024 in the Eastern Panhandle of West Virginia

Supervised and unsupervised classification of land cover change comparison.

Project Overview

What is the problem?

Understanding how land cover has changed between 1984 and 2024 in the Eastern Panhandle of West Virginia, which is the fastest growing region in West Virginia.

Why does it need to be solved?

Understanding how a region is changing can be difficult to really understand given the variety of different jurisdictions and zoning requirements defined by each entity. Seeing how areas are growing can help in understanding factors for environmental management and urban planning for the region as a whole.

Methodology

Collected images from 1984 and 2024 from USGS with less than 5% cloud cover in spring and summer due to the better detection of vegetation.

Images were collected from the middle of September for both years. Different composites were then created using different bands to highlight different aspects of the image.

SWIR composite with 30 figures for each of the 6 classification types were created. These groupings were then used for the supervised classification using the SVM (Support Vector Machine) algorithm, and an NDVI image was added in the algorithm’s classification process.

The next step was to run an unsupervised classification over the same data using an ISODATA clustering algorithm creating 35 clusters that were then grouped together into the 6 land cover types being used for this study. The reclassification was done manually using NDBI and band similarities to help in combining the clusters into the 6 land cover types needed.

Finally, both created image classifications were used together to validate each other. Areas where they different were then reclassified by hand referencing the different composites that have already been used and created for this project such as true color, NDVI, and NDBI.

Validation

Before continuing on with the solution, validation was performed on the image, shown above, to confirm the accuracy of the image classification for the hybrid land cover classification.

Tables 2 and 3 show that accuracy of 80% was not met, and the easiest way to meet that wood was to combine the vegetations and the farm categories together; those results did meet or exceed the 80% required.

Finally, the 1984 image was subtracted by the 2024 image to see how things have changed between those 40 years.
