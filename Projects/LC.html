Land Cover Change from 1984 to 2024 in the Eastern Panhandle of West Virginia

Supervised and unsupervised classification of land cover change comparison.

Project Overview

What is the problem?

Understanding how land cover has changed between 1984 and 2024 in the Eastern Panhandle of West Virginia, which is the fastest growing region in West Virginia.

Why does it need to be solved?

Understanding how a region is changing can be difficult to really understand given the variety of different jurisdictions and zoning requirements defined by each entity. Seeing how areas are growing can help in understanding factors for environmental management and urban planning for the region as a whole.

Methodology

Collected images from 1984 and 2024 from USGS with less than 5% cloud cover in spring and summer due to the better detection of vegetation.

Images were collected from the middle of September for both years. Different composites were then created using different bands to highlight different aspects of the image.

SWIR composite with 30 figures for each of the 6 classification types were created. These groupings were then used for the supervised classification using the SVM (Support Vector Machine) algorithm, and an NDVI image was added in the algorithm’s classification process.

The next step was to run an unsupervised classification over the same data using an ISODATA clustering algorithm creating 35 clusters that were then grouped together into the 6 land cover types being used for this study. The reclassification was done manually using NDBI and band similarities to help in combining the clusters into the 6 land cover types needed.

Finally, both created image classifications were used together to validate each other. Areas where they different were then reclassified by hand referencing the different composites that have already been used and created for this project such as true color, NDVI, and NDBI.

Validation

Before continuing on with the solution, validation was performed on the image, shown above, to confirm the accuracy of the image classification for the hybrid land cover classification.

Tables 2 and 3 show that accuracy of 80% was not met, and the easiest way to meet that wood was to combine the vegetations and the farm categories together; those results did meet or exceed the 80% required.

Finally, the 1984 image was subtracted by the 2024 image to see how things have changed between those 40 years.

Results

The table above shows that while roughly 3,400 acres of forest and almost 5,000 acres of vegetation have changed classification during these 40 years, they only lost 1% each of their total area coverage. The urban classification on the other hand gained 2%, or 9,700 acres, showing that the growth the area is having is causing urban land cover to expand.

Limitations and Conclusions

Limitations

One of the biggest problems in using a raster based approach for land cover classification has to do with the amount of information carried by that one pixel. The analysis was done using a 30 m by 30 m resolution, meaning that a variety of different land cover types could possibly be covered in this area. This is especially true in possible misclassification that can occur in farm areas that have trees boarding their properties or in urban areas that have tree lined streets. The misclassification could also cause salt and pepper in these areas that have more than one land cover type within the 30 m by 30 m area.

The best way to resolve these problems would be to use an image resolution that would represent a smaller area on the ground. While the solution is there, the sharper resolution isn’t always available, especially when looking at older images, so these problems need to be understood as a problem, and results taken with a grain of sand.

Conclusions

Given the results it can be seen as plausible as the growth being seen in the western panhandle being a direct result of loss of vegetation and forest land cover types. The amount of area lost in those two types roughly equal the amount of growth seen by the urban area land cover type.

Future studies in trying to pinpoint land cover changes more directly could be done by reducing the types of land cover types to only 3, rather than the 6 used in the end for this study. The use of only 3 land cover types could help really understand where these changes of land cover were actually occurring on the ground.

Lastly, the one improvement that should have also been performed was running a confusion matrix after each classification, supervised and unsupervised, were originally run rather than waiting until they were combined. Running confusion matrices ensure that SVM for supervised classification and ISODATA for unsupervised classification were working as expected above 80% accuracy by themselves. Also confirming that the training data for the supervised classification was adequate and whether the correct number of clusters were originally created for the unsupervised classification. The final reason this would help would be to see the type of impact combining the classification types together had rather than assuming that combining them together would result in a better overall classification accuracy.
