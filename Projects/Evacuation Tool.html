Pedestrian Evacuation Time Estimator Tool

Everybody has an idea of the tsunami, of being a big wave. It is not a big wave. It is a huge amount of water that comes to land. - Juan Antonio Bayona

Project Overview

What is the problem?

The Pacific Northwest coast faces a well-documented and overdue threat from the Cascadia Subduction Zone, a megathrust fault capable of generating a magnitude 9.0+ earthquake and devastating tsunami. When that event occurs, coastal communities will have minutes to evacuate. Understanding how long it will actually take pedestrians to reach assembly points or high ground, accounting for terrain, land cover, and distance, is a critical need in emergency planning tools.

Why does it need to be solved?

Existing evacuation planning often relies on simplified assumptions about travel time that do not account for slope, land cover type, or the specific distribution of assembly points relative to the population. A tool that models realistic pedestrian travel times allows emergency managers and planners to identify where assembly points are well-positioned, where coverage gaps exist, and which areas of a community face the greatest risk of failing to reach safety within the evacuation window. This information is essential for making evidence-based decisions about where to invest in new assembly points, signage, and evacuation infrastructure.

Tools & Data

Tools
  • Python (arcpy, arcpy.sa, pandas)
  • ArcGIS Pro: Spatial Analyst Extension
  • Custom Geoprocessing Tool (ArcGIS Pro Toolbox)
Data Sources
  • Digital Elevation Model at 10m resolution (used to derive slope)
  • NLCD 2016 Land Cover Raster for Oregon coast study area
  • Land Cover Walking Speed Table (CSV) originally from US Army Corps of Engineers, DOGAMI, and transportation studies
  • Assembly Points (shapefile)
  • Critical Facilities including: police stations, schools, hospitals, fire stations (shapefile)
  • Tsunami Hazard Zone boundary: areas deemed free of tsunami risk (shapefile)

Methodology

This tool was developed as a custom Python geoprocessing tool integrated into ArcGIS Pro, allowing users to run the full evacuation time analysis through a single interface by providing the required inputs. The tool processes data through eight sequential steps:

Step 1: Slope and Tobler Speed

Slope is calculated from the input DEM in percent rise and converted to a fraction. The fraction is then applied to Tobler's Hiking Function, a well-established formula that models how walking speed varies with slope, peaking at a -5% downhill gradient. The result is a raster showing estimated walking speed in meters per minute across the entire study area.

Step 2: Land Cover Speed Reclassification

The land cover raster is reclassified to walking speeds using a user-provided CSV table. Walking speeds for each land cover type were given by the US Army Corps of Engineers, DOGAMI, and transportation studies, reflecting how the average person moves through different terrain types during an emergency, recognizing that evacuees will take any path available to reach safety.

import arcpy
from arcpy import sa
from arcpy.sa import (
    Raster, Reclassify, RemapValue, RemapRange, Con, CellStatistics,
    ExtractByMask, IsNull, Slope, Exp, Abs, CostDistance, CostPath
)
import pandas as pd

arcpy.AddMessage("Starting...")

# Enable Spatial Analyst
arcpy.CheckOutExtension("Spatial")
arcpy.env.overwriteOutput = True

# === Parameters from ArcGIS Tool ===
in_dem = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(0)
in_landcover = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(1)
landcover_speed_t = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(2)
evac_pts = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(3)
start_locations = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(4)
land_zone = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(5)
output_tobler_speed = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(6)
output_lc_speed = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(7)
out_combined_speed = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(8)
output_time_raster = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(9)
output_leastcost_path = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(10)
final_output = arcpy.GetParameterAsText(11)

# === Environment Settings ===
arcpy.env.extent = in_dem
arcpy.env.mask = in_dem

# === Convert Safe Zone to Raster (in memory) ===
land_raster = arcpy.conversion.PolygonToRaster(
    in_features=land_zone,
    value_field="FEATURE",
    cell_assignment="MAXIMUM_AREA",
    priority_field="FEATURE",
    cellsize=arcpy.Describe(in_dem).meanCellWidth
).getOutput(0)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 0 complete: Land zone raster created in memory.")

# === Step 1: Slope and Tobler Speed (m/min) ===
slope_percent = Slope(in_dem, "PERCENT_RISE") / 100.0
tobler_speed = Con(slope_percent > 0, 6000 * Exp(-3.5 * Abs(slope_percent + 0.05)) / 60)
tobler_speed.save(output_tobler_speed)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 1 complete: Tobler speed raster created.")

# === Step 2: Land Cover Reclassification ===
df = pd.read_csv(landcover_speed_t).dropna()
df.columns = df.columns.str.strip()
remap_list = [[int(row[0]), int(row[1])] for row in df.values]
lc_speed = Reclassify(Raster(in_landcover), "Value", RemapValue(remap_list), "NODATA")
lc_speed.save(output_lc_speed)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 2 complete: Land cover reclassified to walking speed.")

# === Step 3: Combine Tobler and Land Cover Speeds ===
combined_speed = Con((lc_speed > 0) & (tobler_speed > 0), CellStatistics([lc_speed, tobler_speed], "MINIMUM", "DATA"))
combined_speed = ExtractByMask(combined_speed, in_dem)
combined_speed.save(out_combined_speed)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 3 complete: Combined walking speed raster saved.")

# === Step 4: Invert Speed to Get Cost Raster ===
cost_raster = Con(combined_speed > 0, 1.0 / combined_speed)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 4 complete: Cost raster (min/m) created.")

# === Step 5: Cost Distance and Time Raster ===
sa.CostDistance(evac_pts, cost_raster, out_backlink_raster="memory/backlink")
backlink = Raster("memory/backlink")
time_evac = sa.CostDistance(evac_pts, cost_raster)
time_evac_safe = Con((time_evac > 0) & (time_evac < 120), time_evac)
time_evac_safe.save(output_time_raster)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 5 complete: Travel time to evacuation points calculated and saved.")

# === Step 6: Least Cost Path Polylines ===
least_cost = CostPath(start_locations, time_evac_safe, backlink, "EACH_ZONE", "FID")
arcpy.RasterToPolyline_conversion(
    in_raster=least_cost,
    out_polyline_features=output_leastcost_path,
    background_value="ZERO",
    simplify="NO_SIMPLIFY"
)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 6 complete: Least cost path polylines saved.")

# === Step 7: Generate Isochrones and Final Classification ===
reclass_map = RemapRange([
    [0, 5, 9], [5, 10, 8], [10, 15, 7], [15, 20, 6],
    [20, 25, 5], [25, 30, 4], [30, 60, 3], [60, 120, 2]
])
iso = Reclassify(time_evac_safe, "Value", reclass_map, "NODATA")
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 7 complete: Isochrone raster created in memory.")

# === Step 8: Final Classification with Tsunami and Safe Land ===
ts = Raster(cost_raster)
land = Raster(land_raster)

base = Con((~IsNull(iso)) & (~IsNull(ts)), iso)
with_tsunami = Con((IsNull(iso)) & (~IsNull(ts)), 0, base)
final = Con(~IsNull(land), 10, with_tsunami)
final.save(final_output)
arcpy.AddMessage("Step 8 complete: Final tsunami impact classification saved.")

Results

Rainfall

   Rainfall shows a strong correlation to the elevation of the terrain. The overall rainfall in one study area along the Reedy River shows that while rainfall varies, a linear trend shows an increase in the amount of rainfall the area will receive in the future.
   Studying the projected flood rates shows a clear trend of increasing rates at 1.5, 10, and 20-year flood rates, with the jump from 2020 to 2040 being a small increase, and the largest jumps being between 2040 and 2080.
   Climate change has made the one-day rainfall amounts show a clear increase, which will only continue to be a problem. In 2005, for example, the rainfall of over 4 inches in one day for 2005 accounted for 10% of the total rainfall for that year. Heavy rainfall is a trend that is expected to continue to increase in frequency and pose a risk regarding flooding with the large volume of water falling over a short period of time.

Temperature

   One of the reasons that precipitation will increase, rainfall intensity will increase, and flow rate will increase stems from an increase in temperature.
   A rate of linear increase of 1°F every 7 years is seen using data from 2000 to 2023.
   The number of days spent above 90°F on average went from about 40 days in the early 2000s to around 60 in the early 2020s. This is a jump of almost a week and a half, dealing with temperatures over 90°F. The same trend is also seen in the number of days seen above 70°F, from around 205 days in the early 2000s to 230 days in the early 2020s; this is almost a full month of warmth.

Land Cover

   The land cover within the watershed shows that forest went from being tied with developed land to now being 10% behind developed land in terms of acres. This change in land cover shows the growth and urban development that is occurring within Greenville County, which can be seen in the maps below, as being mostly in the form of suburban expansion.

Riparian Areas

   Riparian areas were the last items explored as they are those areas around a river that impact the river the most in their contributions to runoff, especially when flooding occurs. Riparian areas are critical in helping understand the health of a river and helping improve the water quality of the river.
   Forests and other vegetation types should be prioritized when looking at the land cover types within the riparian areas. These land cover types provide shade and habitats for fish and other animals that need to rely on the river. Shade is very important for rivers, especially in this watershed, which shows that temperatures are rising, as providing shade helps lower the temperatures of the river. High temperatures can be deadly for animals that live in the Reedy River.
   The actual riparian areas downstream of the city of Greenville show mostly “good” riparian land use, being mostly vegetation and forested. The suburban expansion in the south of the watershed should be limited to continue to help support the river. These good riparian areas downstream of the Greenville help by also providing natural filtering, especially areas like Conestee Nature Preserve, where nature is prioritized, and wetlands were created and are thriving.

Limitations and Conclusions

Limitations

   One of the limitations of the tool is that it assumes that everyone will be walking rather than using any other form of transport, and that everyone will know which direction safety is located.
   The tool also currently has no way to account for travel time assumptions for obstacles, such as cars on the roads, nor the delay with a mass of people moving in the same direction.
   The tool would also benefit from on-ground timing validation to verify that specific travel times, such as the use of Tobler’s Hiking Function and the speeds used going through different land cover types, are accurate.

Conclusions

This tool is not perfect, but it is a basic foundation that could be built upon and expanded. One way the tool could be expanded upon would be through creating different versions of the tool where only vehicle travel times are taken into account, or one that looks into travel time with the use of transit. A final master tool could then use the resulting analysis of all these tools to get a regional average for travel times for a region. This would be a good way to use this tool’s concept for planning for tsunami evacuations.
