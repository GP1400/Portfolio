Project Overview

What is the problem?

Understanding the Reedy River as a whole. Not just its current state, but its history and possible future.

Why does it need to be solved?

The Reedy River is the icon of Greenville, SC, the largest urban area in the state of South Carolina. Understanding its history, and how other areas impact its flow can help make efforts to promote its health and longevity to keep it being an amazing natural feature that people of Greenville know it to be.

Methodology

Researched the history of the Reedy River. Researched Conesstee Conservation Area and its impact on the area, which the Reedy River runs through.

Results

History and Preservation

History shows that many pollutants were thrown into the Reedy River over the years, and the silt built up enough for the Lake Conesstee of a drastic decrease in size drastically from originally having a surface area 145 acres to only 18 acres now.

A new dam was being built to ensure that all these pollutants are secured and that a dam collapse occurs, which would be catastrophic. 2 to 3 million cubic yards of hazardous waste is being protected

Projects like the Conestee Nature Preserve and Unity Park have allowed for wetlands to be restored which the creation of these wetlands “help in flood abatement, naturally filter harmful pollutants from water, store carbon and keep it from being released as a greenhouse gas”

Conestee Nature Preserve has allowed for their area to be deemed an Important Bird Area of Global Significance due to fostering a safe environment for wintering populations of a vulnerable species, the rusty blackbird

Rainfall

Rainfall shows a correlation to elevation, with overall rainfall one study area along the Reedy River showing that while rainfall varies a linear trends shows an increase in the amount of rainfall the area will receive in the future

Studying the projected flood rates show a clear trend of increasing rates at 1.5, 10, and 20 year flood rates, with the jump from 2020 to 2040 being a small increase, with the largest jumps being between 2040 and 2080.

Climate change has made the one day rainfall amounts to show a clear increase, which will only continue to be a problem. In 2005 for example the rain fall of over 4 inches in one day for 2005 accounted for 10% of the total rainfall for that year, these trends are expected to continue to occur moving forward.

Temperature

One of the reasons that precipitation will increase, rainfall intensity will increase, and flow rate will increase all stems from an increase in temperature

Limitations and Conclusions

Limitations

One of the biggest problems in using a raster based approach for land cover classification has to do with the amount of information carried by that one pixel. The analysis was done using a 30 m by 30 m resolution, meaning that a variety of different land cover types could possibly be covered in this area. This is especially true in possible misclassification that can occur in farm areas that have trees boarding their properties or in urban areas that have tree lined streets. The misclassification could also cause salt and pepper in these areas that have more than one land cover type within the 30 m by 30 m area.

The best way to resolve these problems would be to use an image resolution that would represent a smaller area on the ground. While the solution is there, the sharper resolution isn’t always available, especially when looking at older images, so these problems need to be understood as a problem, and results taken with a grain of sand.

Conclusions

Given the results it can be seen as plausible as the growth being seen in the western panhandle being a direct result of loss of vegetation and forest land cover types. The amount of area lost in those two types roughly equal the amount of growth seen by the urban area land cover type.

Future studies in trying to pinpoint land cover changes more directly could be done by reducing the types of land cover types to only 3, rather than the 6 used in the end for this study. The use of only 3 land cover types could help really understand where these changes of land cover were actually occurring on the ground.

Lastly, the one improvement that should have also been performed was running a confusion matrix after each classification, supervised and unsupervised, were originally run rather than waiting until they were combined. Running confusion matrices ensure that SVM for supervised classification and ISODATA for unsupervised classification were working as expected above 80% accuracy by themselves. Also confirming that the training data for the supervised classification was adequate and whether the correct number of clusters were originally created for the unsupervised classification. The final reason this would help would be to see the type of impact combining the classification types together had rather than assuming that combining them together would result in a better overall classification accuracy.
