Hydrology of the Reedy River within Greenville County

"A river is the report card for its watershed." - Alan Levere, environmentalist and educator, from his speeches.

Project Overview

What is the Reedy River?

The Reedy River begins its journey just north of Travelers Rest, South Carolina, flowing through Greenville County before connecting with Lake Greenwood in neighboring Laurens County. The river spans approximately 65 miles with a drainage basin of 402 mi² and sits within the greater Saluda River HUC-8 watershed. Despite being the defining natural feature of the largest urban area in South Carolina, the river has a complex and troubled history of pollution, industrialization, and neglect that continues to shape its health today.

Why study it?

Understanding the Reedy River requires looking at more than its current state. Its history of industrial pollution, the ongoing sedimentation crisis at Lake Conestee, and the accelerating urbanization of Greenville County all directly affect the river's health and future. Climate change contributes to this through increased rainfall intensity, rising temperatures, and more frequent floods. A comprehensive look at the river's hydrology, land cover, and riparian areas can help guide preservation and rehabilitation efforts to protect this irreplaceable natural and civic resource.

Methodology

This project takes a comprehensive look at the hydrology of the Reedy River within Greenville County through five areas of analysis, along with a watershed delineation to identify sub-catchments for potential future study.

  • History & Preservation

    A literature review of the river's history of industrial pollution, legal battles, and rehabilitation efforts, drawing from Friends of the Reedy River, Conestee Nature Preserve, Unity Park, and the Lake Conestee Dam Restoration Project.

  • Weather & Water Flow

    Historical precipitation data from NOAA analyzed alongside future projections from the MACA Data Portal. Stream flow data from USGS assessed current and projected flood rates at three locations: Greenville, Lake Conestee, and the end of the study area.

  • Temperature

    Average annual temperature data from 2000 to 2023 analyzed using NOAA Climate Data Online to identify warming trends and increases in extreme heat days.

  • Land Cover

    Land cover data from MRLC analyzed for 2000 and 2023 to identify changes across the watershed. Land cover types were reclassified into broader categories to better visualize transitions between types over time.

  • Riparian Areas

    The riparian zone was analyzed using reclassified land cover data, classifying areas as either favorable (forest and vegetation) or unfavorable (developed and barren) to identify areas of concern and rehabilitation success.

  • Watershed Delineation

    Watershed delineation was performed in ArcGIS Pro to identify specific sub-catchments within the greater Reedy River watershed for potential future study.

Results

History and Preservation

   The Reedy River has a long and complex history of industrial use and pollution. By the 1900s, eight mills were operating on the river within two miles of downtown Greenville, and the river was used for disposing of textile mill waste. This pollution led to Conestee Mills suing the city of Greenville in 1925 over the damage being done to their operations. By the time the case was ruled on, in 1931, the city of Greenville had already built its first wastewater treatment plant in 1928, helping to alleviate these problems.
   The decades of industrial activity left a lasting mark. The amount of silt and sediment that built up behind the Conestee Dam reduced Lake Conestee from its original 145 acres to less than 18 acres today. Efforts are currently underway to rebuild the dam to prevent two to three million cubic yards of hazardous sediment from flowing into the river in the event of a dam failure.
   Despite this history, significant rehabilitation efforts have been made to help improve the conditions of the river. Conestee Nature Preserve, a 640-acre wildlife sanctuary, has restored native habitats and wetlands that support far more species than the area originally did. Unity Park has restored 8 acres of wetlands along the river's original pre-1933 path, which helps in flood abatement, naturally filters harmful pollutants, and stores carbon. Conestee Nature Preserve has even been designated an Important Bird Area of Global Significance, fostering safe wintering habitat for the rusty blackbird, a vulnerable species that depends on the healthy wetland conditions the preserve has worked to create.

Rainfall

   Precipitation in the Reedy River watershed shows a strong correlation with elevation; the higher the terrain, the more rainfall the area receives. Looking at the past 25 years, annual rainfall has typically ranged between 40 and 50 inches, with a linear trend showing a gradual increase over time. Projections suggest this trend will continue, with average annual rainfall rising from below 48 inches currently to over 50 inches by 2040 and over 50.5 inches by 2070.
   As precipitation increases, so do flow rates. Stream flow projections at three locations along the river, Greenville, Lake Conestee, and the end of the study area, show mean flow rates increasing from historic averages through 2040. Perhaps most concerning is that what is currently a 25-year flood event is projected to become a 10-year flood event by 2040, meaning major flooding will occur far more frequently than it does today.
   The intensity of individual rainfall events is also increasing. While rainfall of 4 inches in a single day has only occurred four times in the past 25 years, trend lines show single-day rainfall amounts increasing from around 3 inches to close to 3.5 inches. To put this in perspective, the 4 inches of rain that fell in a single day in 2005 accounted for nearly 10% of that entire year's rainfall, meaning just 10 days of that intensity would equal a full year's worth of rain.

Temperature

   Rising temperatures are a key driver behind the increasing precipitation, rainfall intensity, and flow rates seen in the Reedy River watershed. As temperatures rise, more water vapor enters the atmosphere, allowing for more intense storms and heavier downpours.
   Average annual temperature data from 2000 to 2023 shows a clear linear increase of approximately 1°F every 7 years. While temperatures fluctuate from year to year, the overall trend is consistent. Years like 2016, 2017, and 2019 have been the warmest on record for this area, and if the current rate of increase holds, 2026 is projected to become the new expected average temperature.
   This warming trend is also reflected in the number of extreme heat days. The number of days above 90°F increased from around 40 in the early 2000s to around 60 in the early 2020s, nearly a week and a half more of extreme heat annually. Similarly, the number of days above 70°F grew from around 205 to 230, which is almost a full extra month of warm temperatures compared to just two decades ago.

Land Cover

   Land cover within the Reedy River watershed changed significantly between 2000 and 2023, driven primarily by suburban expansion in the southern portions of Greenville County. While the central city of Greenville saw only modest increases in development intensity over this period, the surrounding suburban areas experienced dramatic transitions from rural landscapes to low and medium-intensity development.
   The most telling shift is seen in the balance between forest and developed land. In 2000, forest and developed land were nearly equal in coverage across the watershed. By 2023, developed land had grown to exceed forest cover by 10 percentage points, a significant loss of natural land cover in just over two decades. The biggest land cover transitions were forest to developed and farmland to developed, both of which reflect the suburban growth pattern being experienced across Greenville County.
   Land cover types like open water, wetlands, and natural vegetation remained relatively stable over the same period, suggesting that while urbanization is expanding, the most sensitive natural areas have so far been largely preserved.

Riparian Areas

   Riparian areas, the land immediately surrounding a river, have the biggest impact on a river's health, specifically in their contributions to runoff and water quality during flooding events. Understanding the land cover within the riparian zone of the Reedy River helps identify where the river is most vulnerable and where rehabilitation efforts are having the greatest impact.
   Forest and vegetation are the most beneficial riparian land cover types. They provide shade that helps regulate river temperatures, which is important in a watershed where temperatures are rising, but also in creating critical habitats for fish and other wildlife that depend on the river. Developed and barren land cover, in comparison, only provides faster runoff, higher flood peaks, and increased water temperatures that can be harmful and even deadly to aquatic life.
   The riparian areas at the upper end of the watershed, near the start of the river, are dominated by unfavorable land cover, developed and barren areas that lack the protective benefits of natural vegetation. However, the riparian areas downstream of Greenville show mostly favorable land cover, with forest and vegetation providing natural filtering and temperature regulation. Areas like Conestee Nature Preserve, where wetlands have been restored, and nature is prioritized, represent the kind of riparian management that should be expanded further upstream to help protect and rehabilitate the Reedy River as a whole.

Limitations and Conclusions

Limitations

   The most significant limitation of this study is the lack of physical data collected directly from the river itself. Water quality measurements, including concentrations of organic and inorganic materials, E. coli, and fecal coliform levels, would be needed to truly validate the findings presented here and to better understand the health of the native wildlife that depends on the Reedy River. These tests would need to be performed at multiple locations along the river and over an extended time period to confirm consistency and identify specific areas of concern.
   The climate and flow rate models used in this analysis also present limitations. The reasoning behind specific projected numbers is not always transparent in the models themselves, and applying models at a smaller, more localized scale would provide better estimates for the future of the Reedy River, particularly when it comes to projected flow rates. Additionally, this study used the RCP 4.5 climate scenario, which represents a middle-ground projection for climate change rather than the more extreme RCP 8.5 scenario. This should be taken into account when applying these findings; RCP 8.5 would be more appropriate for infrastructure and flood planning near the river, while RCP 4.5 better represents what everyday conditions are likely to look like.

Conclusions

   The Reedy River has come a long way from its days as an industrial waste dumping ground. Understanding its history of pollution and neglect helps explain the current state of its health and shows a clear path to continue improving its conditions. The rehabilitation efforts already underway, through Conestee Nature Preserve, Unity Park, and the Lake Conestee Dam Restoration Project, demonstrate that recovery is possible when communities invest in their waterways.
   The data presented in this study paints a concerning picture for the future, however. Rising temperatures, increasing rainfall intensity, and higher projected flood rates all point to growing pressure on the river and the communities that depend on it. The suburbanization of Greenville County is further compounding these pressures by reducing the forest and vegetation cover that helps protect the river's riparian zone.
   Moving forward, a collaboration between the counties and cities that rely on the Reedy River would be beneficial in helping preserve and protect it from the unintended consequences of continued urbanization. Improving riparian land cover types along the river, expanding wetland restoration efforts, and conducting regular water quality testing would all be meaningful next steps. Even smaller efforts, such as picking up trash along the riverbank, have a measurable impact on how the river is perceived and used by both humans and wildlife alike.
