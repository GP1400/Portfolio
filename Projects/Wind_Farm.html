Wind Farm Suitability Analysis for West Virginia

"Solar power, wind power, the way forward is to collaborate with nature - it's the only way we are going to get to the other end of the 21st century." - Björk

Project Overview

What is the problem?

West Virginia is known for its power production capabilities, but with 95% of its energy still coming from coal, its reliance on fossil fuels leaves it vulnerable as the world shifts toward renewable energy. Wind power is perfectly suited to be used in West Virginia's mountainous terrain, making it a great alternative. The problem would then become identifying where wind farms can actually be placed to generate power effectively and responsibly.

Why does it need to be solved?

Wind farms cannot be placed anywhere. Three broad categories of factors need to be taken into consideration for them to be successful: environmental constraints, human acceptability, and economic viability. These factors together limit where a wind farm can be placed to be successful and maximize energy production, while minimizing conflict with nature, communities, and infrastructure costs.

Methodology

   This project uses a Multiple Criteria Decision-Making Analysis (MCDA) framework with equal weighting across all criteria, avoiding expert-bias-driven approaches such as the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), which tends to prioritize economic viability over environmental and social factors. Suitable sites were identified through a review of existing wind farm suitability literature and were organized into three categories: environmental constraints, human acceptability, and economic viability.

Environmental Constraints

   Bird and bat mortality had historically been a concern with wind farm development. While improvements have reduced mortality rates, a 1 km exclusion buffer was still applied around important bird areas, as designated by the DNR, as a result of historic precautions.
   Parks and protected lands were fully excluded with a 2 km buffer, and water bodies were excluded with a 1 km buffer to protect wetlands and prevent hazardous runoff from wind farm construction and operation.

Human Acceptability

   Human opposition is frequently cited as the primary reason wind farm projects are denied. Taking neighboring communities into account is therefore a critical part of responsible wind farm siting.
   Urban areas, identified through land cover classification, were assigned a linear weighted buffer to account for urban proximity and the visual impacts it could have on communities.
   An airport buffer was used around airports as recommended by the FAA of 3.5 miles or ~5.6 km to prevent interference with flight paths and radar systems.

Economic Viability

   Wind speeds are the primary driver of energy production and profitability, so only areas with a minimum wind speed of 5 m/s were considered for this study.
   Terrain slope was capped at 15% to keep construction costs down. West Virginia's mountainous terrain made this threshold particularly important as steeper slopes significantly increase site preparation costs.
   Proximity to existing infrastructure - roads, railroads, and transmission lines - was prioritized using linear weighted buffers, as proximity reduces construction costs and the expense of connecting the wind farm to the grid.

Infrastructure Proximity

   Roads, railroads, and transmission lines were each assigned linear weighted buffers with a maximum distance of 10,000 m. A minimum buffer was also applied around transmission lines and railroads to account for the diameter span of wind turbine blades.

All criteria were then combined using an equal-weight MCDA, ensuring no single factor dominated the final suitability result.

Results

   The analysis identified approximately 514 mi2 of West Virginia as having excellent suitability for wind farm development, with an additional 1,118 mi2 rated very good and 974 mi2 rated good. Combined, this means that roughly 13% of the state meets the suitability criteria established in this study, with the strongest concentrations found in the Allegheny Highlands and the Eastern Panhandle.
   When compared against the only other wind farm suitability study conducted for West Virginia, by Ajanaku et al. (2022), there was notable overlap despite the two studies using different weighting approaches. That study, which used an expert-driven Analytic Hierarchy Process prioritizing economic factors, identified Tucker, Grant, Mineral, and Pocahontas counties as most suitable. This study identified Grant, Mineral, Upshur, Barbour, Present, and western Randolph counties as suitable. This means that Grant and Mineral counties were deemed suitable by both analyses, lending additional confidence to those findings.
   Overlaying existing wind farm locations onto the final suitability map showed that while some turbines fall within suitable areas, others do not - suggesting that real-world development decisions involve additional site-specific considerations beyond the broad criteria used for this analysis.

Limitations and Conclusions

Limitations

   One of the biggest limitations in this analysis is within those binary exclusion areas, which might have been better represented with a gradient ranking to allow for more nuance in borderline areas. Equal weighting across all criteria is another limitation, as it may not fully reflect real-world development priorities. While a weighted approach such as the Analytic Hierarchy Process could address this, it introduces expert bias - which is why it was avoided in this study. Future analyses could explore a ranked weight approach or a Pairwise comparison method to better distribute importance across criteria without relying on expert opinion.
   One of the broader challenges in wind farm suitability studies is the integration of social factors into the decision-making process. Human opposition is frequently cited as the primary reason wind farm projects are denied, yet it remains difficult to quantify spatially. Better mechanisms for incorporating local public input, alongside public education on the benefits of wind energy, would strengthen future suitability analyses. Notably, siting wind farms on previously undesirable land uses - such as landfills or strip mines - has shown promise in gaining public acceptance.

Conclusions

   This analysis shows that 13% of West Virginia has strong potential for wind farm development, concentrated in the Allegheny Highlands and Eastern Panhandle. While not all existing wind farms fall within the identified suitable areas, this is consistent with the broader literature, which shows that real-world siting decisions often prioritize economic factors over the broader criteria used in regional suitability studies.
   Given the overlap between this study and Ajanaku et al. (2022) in Grant and Mineral counties, these areas represent the strongest candidates for future investigation. A follow-up county-level study incorporating land use and zoning data could provide the granular detail needed to guide responsible wind farm development - and help West Virginia continue its legacy as an energy powerhouse, now powered by the wind.
