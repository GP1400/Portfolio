Landslide Susceptibility Hot Spot Analysis for West Virginia

"Even with good maps, there's no guarantee that the public will get the word about landslide hazards, or that state and local governments will take action to discourage or prevent building in dangerous areas." — Bill Dedman

Project Overview

What is the problem?

Landslides are a significant hazard across West Virginia, a state that’s characterized by steep topography and vulnerable communities. The rapid movement of soil, rock, and debris downslope causes extensive damage and loss of life, which disproportionately affects those least able to recover. Landslides can be triggered by intense rainfall, seismic activity, and human-induced landscape alterations. While landslide susceptibility maps exist for West Virginia, understanding where high-risk areas are spatially concentrated could help in hazard management and resource allocation.

Why does it need to be solved?

Landslides are not randomly distributed across the landscape, as they tend to cluster in areas shaped by underlying environmental factors like slope, soil properties, land cover, and hydrologic conditions. Identifying where these clusters occur statistically, rather than just visually, allows for more defensible decisions about where to focus funding, infrastructure investment, and mitigation efforts. Climate change further increases the urgency of this work by altering precipitation patterns and increasing soil moisture variability in areas already prone to instability.

Methodology

   A raster of landslide susceptibility for West Virginia was created as part of an ongoing project using LiDAR-based digital elevation model variables at a two-meter resolution (Maxwell, 2021; Maxwell, 2020). While this resolution is excellent for site-level analysis, identifying broader spatial patterns required a different approach. The solution was to convert the raster into a vector format by summarizing mean landslide susceptibility values by HUC-10 watershed, a spatial unit that naturally accounts for both topography and water flow, the two primary factors influencing landslide occurrence.
   Watersheds were chosen over the more commonly used county boundaries because counties are administrative units that do not reflect the natural processes that drive landslide risk. HUC-10 watersheds, by contrast, capture where water flows and where terrain creates conditions for slope failure, making them a more meaningful unit for this type of analysis.

   HUC-10 Watersheds of West Virginia. The 228 HUC-10 watersheds were used as the spatial unit of analysis for this study. Watersheds were selected over county boundaries because they represent the natural processes that drive landslide risk, topography and water flow.

This study applies three levels of spatial statistics, moving from global to local in scale:
  • Global Statistics — Two global tests were applied to understand overall spatial patterns across the state. Global Moran's I tested whether watersheds with similar landslide susceptibility values tend to be located near each other, in other words, whether the data shows spatial autocorrelation. The Getis-Ord General G test then assessed whether the clustering was driven by high susceptibility values, low susceptibility values, or both. For both tests, neighbors were defined using "contiguity edges, corners," meaning any watershed sharing a border, node, or overlap was considered a neighbor, which reflects how water and terrain connect adjacent watersheds.
  • Multi-Distance Statistics — Ripley's K Function was applied to identify the spatial scale at which clustering occurs, testing distances from 10 km to 150 km in 10 km increments. Since Ripley's K requires point data, the centroids of watersheds with z-scores greater than one were used rather than all watershed centroids. A simulation boundary with 99 iterations was used to account for edge effects near the state border.
  • Local Statistics — Finally, Getis-Ord Gi* was applied to identify statistically significant hot spots and cold spots at the individual watershed level. This local statistic assigns each watershed a z-score indicating whether it is surrounded by similarly high or low susceptibility values, with results classified into confidence levels of 90%, 95%, and 99%.

Global Statistics

   Global Moran's I showed a very strong positive spatial autocorrelation in landslide susceptibility values across HUC-10 watersheds (I = 0.72, z = 15.19, p < 0.001), confirming that watersheds with similar susceptibility values tend to be located near one another. A Moran's I value near positive one indicates strong clustering, which the result of 0.72, represents a high degree of spatial dependence across the state.
   The Getis-Ord General G test indicated significant global clustering of high landslide susceptibility values (z = 6.26, p < 0.001), suggesting that areas of high susceptibility are regionally concentrated rather than randomly distributed. The positive z-score specifically indicates that the clustering is driven by high susceptibility values rather than low ones.

Multi-Distance Statistics

Ripley's K Function confirmed that statistically significant clustering of high landslide susceptibility watersheds occurs at distances ranging from approximately 30 km to 140 km. This is identified on the graph where the observed K value rises above the upper confidence envelope. Below 30 km and above 140 km, the pattern approaches spatial randomness. The result means more broadly that the clustering is regional rather than purely a local phenomenon. This finding guided the scale at which the local hot spot analysis was applied.

Local Statistics

   The Getis-Ord Gi* results revealed two distinct hot spot clusters and two cold spot clusters across the state.
   Hot spots, watersheds with high landslide susceptibility surrounded by similarly high susceptibility watersheds, are concentrated on the eastern side of West Virginia. The southeastern point of the state contains the first hot spot cluster, with seven watersheds at 99% confidence, five surrounding watersheds at 95% confidence, and two at 90% confidence. With only a single watershed separating them, the second hot spot cluster sits just south of the northern panhandle, containing nine watersheds at 99% confidence, six at 95% confidence, and six at 90% confidence.
   Cold spots, watersheds with low susceptibility surrounded by similarly low susceptibility watersheds, were most pronounced in the Eastern Panhandle, where thirteen watersheds show 99% confidence, two show 95% confidence, and five show 90% confidence. This concentration of 99% confidence cold spot watersheds with fewer surrounding lower-confidence watersheds indicates a particularly well-defined cold spot cluster. A smaller secondary cold spot exists in the southern interior of the state, with two watersheds at 95% confidence surrounded by six at 90% confidence.
   Together, these findings confirm that landslide susceptibility in West Virginia is not randomly distributed but follows strong regional patterns that align with the state's underlying terrain and hydrologic characteristics.

Limitations and Conclusions

Limitations

   While using HUC-10 watersheds rather than county boundaries made sense for understanding the natural processes driving landslide clustering, county-level vectors would be more practical for translating these findings into funding allocations and policy decisions. A future study combining both spatial units could help bridge the gap between natural process understanding and administrative decision-making.
   The landslide susceptibility raster used in this study was created exclusively from LiDAR-derived DEM variables, which is a limitation when trying to fully understand landslide susceptibility clustering risk. Factors like soil properties, land cover, vegetation, and precipitation patterns, which also influence landslide susceptibility, were not included in the original raster and therefore not captured in this clustering analysis.
   Finally, this study does not include a landslide inventory to validate the clustering results against actual recorded landslide events. Validating the hot spot findings against a real landslide inventory would significantly strengthen the conclusions and provide a more direct measure of the analysis accuracy.

Conclusions

   This study demonstrates that landslide susceptibility in West Virginia is strongly spatially autocorrelated, regionally clustered, and scale-dependent. Results consistently showed that susceptibility is not randomly distributed but instead follows strong regional patterns shaped by the state's underlying terrain and hydrologic characteristics.
   The combined use of Global Moran's I, Getis-Ord General G, Ripley's K Function, and Getis-Ord Gi* provides a comprehensive analytical framework for understanding both the intensity and spatial organization of landslide susceptibility. The progression from global to multi-distance to local statistics allowed for a thorough characterization of spatial patterns at multiple scales, something that no single statistical method could achieve alone.
   The hot spot findings have direct practical implications. The two hot spot clusters identified, in the southeastern point of the state and just south of the northern panhandle, represent areas where funding for landslide mitigation and hazard management should be prioritized. Climate change is likely to intensify these risks further by altering precipitation patterns and increasing soil moisture variability in areas already prone to instability. The cold spot areas, particularly the Eastern Panhandle, represent comparatively lower risk areas where development may be less hazardous relative to the rest of the state.
